## Gate spec

The right gate order is **provenance first, structure second, rendered conformance third, human validation fourth**. In practice, that means keeping the existing spine / IA-evidence gates exactly where they are, then adding a dedicated **IA-structure gate** at artifact time, then an **IA-render conformance gate** at build/render time, and only then running the bounded ASK suite. This sequencing matches the literature: card sorting informs structure before implementation, tree testing validates hierarchy before visual design, and comprehension must be confirmed on realistic tasks rather than inferred from citations or polish. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn37view1Ņłéturn20view3Ņłéturn32view0Ņłü

At **artifact time**, Cartographer should run blockers for the defects that are both high-impact and genuinely machine-detectable. The blocker list should include: critical terminology drift; concept-label collisions; orphan pages and dangling entity references; missing list/detail homes for core entities; child-bearing categories with no section overview or landing page; canonical states without deep links; critical task paths with unrecoverable dead ends; active-nav/breadcrumb ancestry mismatches in the route specification; missing required metadata on core entities; and explicit junk-drawer categories in primary navigation. These are pre-build structural defects, not design opinions, and they map cleanly to the artifact model plus vocabulary extraction. ## Eyes-only residue

The residue that only humans can verify is smaller than teams often fear, but it is still real. The first residue class is **semantic fit of ambiguous categories**: whether users truly believe two things belong together, whether a category boundary feels natural, and whether a label uses the audienceŌĆÖs language rather than the teamŌĆÖs internal shorthand. Open and closed card sorts remain the right practice here. Machine clustering can predict overlap, but it cannot prove the meaning of the grouping for a lived audience. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn20view5Ņłéturn37view1Ņłéturn39view0Ņłü

The second residue class is **critical-task findability under uncertainty**: which branch a first-time user will choose when several plausible routes exist. Tree tests and first-click tests remain mandatory here. NN/g explicitly uses first-click data to judge category-name strength, and MeasuringUŌĆÖs first-click effect is directionally strong enough to justify explicit gating, but the 2025 validation study also shows that tree-test method choice changes results. That means models may simulate this question, but only humans can settle it for launch. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn38view2Ņłéturn33view2Ņłéturn23view4Ņłü

The third residue class is **blind comprehension of task-defining copy and IA-shaped summaries**. When labels, overview text, decision cards, empty states, or status summaries carry structural meaning, comprehension must be measured by recognition, recall, and recounting, not by asking people whether they ŌĆ£understood.ŌĆØ This is the right home for the Hall-like failure class in your motivating case: if a first-time viewer cannot correctly explain what a card means, what system they are in, or what happens next, provenance and structure were not enough. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn32view0Ņłéturn32view1Ņłü

The fourth residue class is **mental-model acceptability of novel structures**. A machine can compare a product to an inbox, editor, dashboard, catalog, or checkout pattern and flag novelty. It cannot honestly determine whether a bespoke structure has earned the right to exist. That is what qualitative task-based testing is for. Launching a novel top-level model without ASK is effectively betting that the teamŌĆÖs explanation and the userŌĆÖs explanation will coincide. The literature does not support that bet. In short, the named ASK scope is: **category meaning, first-click findability, blind comprehension, and novel mental-model fit**. Everything else should be forced as early as possible into deterministic artifact-time or render-time checks. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn20view3Ņłéturn20view5Ņłéturn32view0Ņłü

## What didnŌĆÖt survive

The **three-click rule** does not survive verification. NN/g calls it an ŌĆ£unofficial heuristicŌĆØ and says it ŌĆ£has not been supported by data.ŌĆØ Click count is too blunt to capture information scent, grouping quality, detours, or recovery cost. For Guild, this means depth may be measured, but it must never be gated by a magic maximum click count. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn33view0Ņłü

A **magic menu-size number** does not survive either. Classic depth/breadth work found a controlled optimum near two levels of eight choices, but later work and modern guidance show that performance depends on label quality, context visibility, hierarchy asymmetry, viewport constraints, and domain expectations. The right operational move is to measure hierarchy shape and then confirm critical tasks with tree testing, not to freeze a universal menu cap. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn23view0Ņłéturn23view1Ņłéturn20view7Ņłü

The claim that **shallow always beats deep** also fails. The literature leans against unnecessary depth, but it simultaneously warns that overwide or poorly labeled structures create their own failures. Even expandable structures can lose when they create long, hard-to-scan lists. Guild should therefore treat depth and breadth as interacting variables, not as moral categories. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn23view1Ņłéturn20view7Ņłü

The claim that **search can replace navigation** fails. NN/g lists ŌĆ£search and structure not integratedŌĆØ as a top IA mistake; BaymardŌĆÖs results show that search-versus-browse usage changes substantially by domain; and no-results pages become abandonment points when the browse structure and recovery routes are weak. The claim that **card sorting gives you the final IA** fails. Donna Spencer explicitly says card sorting is formative input, not an evaluation of what is wrong with the existing site and not a silver bullet. NN/gŌĆÖs distinction between card sorting and tree testing reinforces this: one uncovers grouping expectations, the other validates a proposed hierarchy. Guild should treat card sorts as schema input and tree tests as structure validation, never as interchangeable proof. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn37view1Ņłéturn20view6Ņłü

The claim that **progressive disclosure is always good** fails. NN/g recommends it for advanced or rarely used features, but the site-map evidence shows that when primary content is hidden behind disclosure users do not understand, the structure becomes effectively unusable. Guild should gate not the presence of disclosure, but whether disclosure hides primary meaning or mandatory next steps. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn20view11Ņłéturn18view0Ņłéturn19view0Ņłü

The idea that **polyhierarchy is either always good or always bad** also fails. NN/g explicitly recommends it for ambiguous IA categories and multiple mental models, but warns that too many parents increase cognitive load. This is a real contradiction in practice only if teams demand a single dogma. The evidence says to use polyhierarchy sparingly, in response to ambiguity, and to validate it with users.
