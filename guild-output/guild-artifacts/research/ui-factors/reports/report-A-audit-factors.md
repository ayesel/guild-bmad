# Operational UI Audit Taxonomy for AI Agents

## Verdict

A complete rendered-surface audit space is broader than accessibility plus geometry. It has three layers: **can I perceive and operate it**, **can I instantly parse what matters and what belongs together**, and **does the surface feel honest, coherent, and credible at first glance and after interaction**. Guild already covers much of the first layer and some of the second. The single highest-leverage remaining gap in a contrast-plus-geometry audit is **hierarchy-plus-signifiers**: whether the eye lands on the right action first, and whether interactive things unmistakably announce themselves. NN/gÔÇÖs hierarchy research says hierarchy should ÔÇťguide the eye to the most important elements,ÔÇŁ and its clickability research says interactive components ÔÇťmust retain sufficient cuesÔÇŁ; weak signifiers measurably increase uncertainty and effort. That is exactly the class of failure owners often spot instantly while deterministic audits still pass. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search3ţłéturn19search1ţłéturn19search16ţłéturn23search3ţłü

## What didnÔÇÖt survive

Several common design claims did **not** survive adversarial verification as hard gates.

The first casualty is the popular idea that **HickÔÇÖs law gives a universal maximum number of choices**. The modern review of Hick-Hyman law is much more careful, and the review literature explicitly argues against using it as a simplistic ÔÇťless is betterÔÇŁ design rule. NN/g likewise warns there is no usability gain in blindly limiting menus to seven items because menus rely on recognition, not recall.
