# Personas

## Maya the Juggler

Maya is a parent of two, works a hybrid operations role, and keeps home logistics in three places: a family calendar, a work task tracker, and a running notes app. She is not trying to become a quantified-self power user. She wants a quick way to keep small routines visible when the day changes.

### Goals

- See which habits are still recoverable today.
- Separate family, health, and admin routines without building a complex project system.
- Avoid guilt-heavy language when a streak breaks.
- Add a new habit in under a minute.

### Frictions

- Calendar blocks move when school or work changes.
- Existing habit apps treat missed days as failure instead of rescheduling.
- Dashboards with too many charts slow her down.

### Design Implications

- Keep the dashboard glanceable and row-based.
- Show next cue language in human terms, not only time.
- Use recovery states and muted copy instead of warning-heavy styling.

## Dev the Checklister

Dev co-parents, freelances on two active projects, and uses checklists to manage recurring responsibilities. They like clear progress, but they abandon tools that require too much setup. They often review habits at night and decide what to carry into tomorrow.

### Goals

- Track cross-project routines in one place.
- Know which streaks are active without opening each habit.
- Create routines with a cue and cadence rather than a long form.
- Use the app comfortably on a laptop or phone.

### Frictions

- Generic to-do apps bury recurring habit history.
- Wellness apps feel too soft and promotional.
- Overly playful completion effects feel distracting.

### Design Implications

- Use compact cards with explicit labels and restrained completion indicators.
- Favor semantic badges over decorative illustration.
- Keep motion short, predictable, and optional.
