# Brand Strategy & Verbal Identity Reference

Grounded reference for the Enchanter's strategy and verbal-identity work. Strategy is the spine: every visual and verbal decision downstream must trace back to it.

## The Load-Bearing Principle

- **Strategy must drive execution.** Personas, competitive analyses, journey maps, and attribute lists are *tools, not ends*. They only have value if they meaningfully shape how the brand is made manifest. "Strategy without execution is toothless." (Pentagram)
- This is the line between brand work and generalist UI work: a senior brand designer never produces a strategy artifact that doesn't change a downstream visual/verbal decision.
- Brand strategy = the deliberate decisions defining **what a brand stands for, who it serves, how it differentiates, and how it consistently presents across every context.**

## Positioning

- The **positioning statement is the most compact expression of the entire brand strategy.** If it's vague, everything downstream is vague.
- **Real differentiation claims something specific that competitors either can't claim or don't claim.** "Innovative," "trusted," and "customer-focused" are not differentiation — every company claims them.
- Test: could a competitor put their name on this positioning statement and have it still be true? If yes, it isn't positioning.

## Brand Attributes & Archetypes

- **Brand attributes**: 3–5 adjectives that act as a filter for every later decision (does this logo / color / sentence feel `<attribute>`?).
- **Jungian archetypes** (Hero, Caregiver, Explorer, Creator, Sage, Outlaw, Magician, etc.) give a shared *personality vocabulary*. Frame with: "if this brand were a person, how would they behave and communicate?"
- Archetype is a vocabulary, not a cage — it aligns the team on personality, then strategy specializes it.

## Audience & Emotional Mood

- Define who the brand serves and the *emotional register* it should evoke. This becomes the brief for visual mood (color temperature, type personality, illustration tone) and verbal voice.

## Verbal Identity — Voice vs. Tone

- **Voice = the consistent personality reflected in all writing.** It does not change. (PatternFly / Red Hat)
- **Tone = the context-dependent emotional approach** — accounts for the user's situation and adapts (an error vs. a celebration). "Voice is the climate; tone is the weather."
- Brand-level voice is expressed as a small set of **voice traits**, each an extension of a brand attribute (Red Hat maps "Helpful-but-humble → Friendly," "Brave-but-balanced → Inventive," etc.).

## The Enchanter ↔ Warlock Boundary (critical — prevents scope bloat)

The seam where brand verbal identity ends and product copy begins:

- **Enchanter owns the brand voice SYSTEM** — the voice traits, the do/don't tone matrix, naming, tagline, the verbal personality.
- **Warlock owns product/UX copy** that *inherits and applies* that system — microcopy, error messages, empty states, button labels.
- The handoff is a **derivation**: brand voice traits → product UX voice traits (PatternFly does exactly this). Warlock never invents new voice; Enchanter never writes product microcopy.
- One-line contract: *Enchanter sets the voice; Warlock speaks in it.*

## Deliverables (what the strategy phase outputs)

- Positioning statement (1 sentence)
- Brand attributes (3–5) + chosen archetype
- Audience + emotional mood note
- Verbal identity: voice traits, tone matrix, naming/tagline (if in scope)
- All of the above feed `brand-identity.md` as the brand *intent* that seeds downstream visual craft and the Claude Design onboarding (see `brand-system-and-handoff-reference`).
